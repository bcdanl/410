Exploring Socioeconomic Factors Associated with Low Food Access Tracts in New York State

Cameron & Nada

Author

Byeong-Hak Choe

Published

March 20, 2026

Hi Cameron and Nada,

Thank you for your Research Kick-off Report. You have chosen a topic with real policy importance. Food access is closely tied to nutrition, transportation, neighborhood opportunity, and health, and New York State gives you meaningful variation between dense urban areas, suburbs, small cities, and rural counties. Starting with the USDA Food Access Research Atlas is appropriate, and a binary-response model is a sensible method if your outcome is whether a tract is classified as low access.

The proposal is clearly motivated and well organized. The main priorities for your capstone paper are to choose whether the primary goal is inference or prediction, define the USDA outcome variable with greater precision, and handle the urban and rural structure of the data more carefully. You should also tighten the model specification, be more explicit about what the USDA variables do and do not measure, and clarify whether your dataset is truly cross-sectional or a short tract-level panel created by combining multiple USDA releases.

Next Steps

You are working toward both the presentation and the capstone paper. Focus on a clear empirical goal, a carefully defined tract-level model, and careful interpretation of what the data can and cannot tell us.

  • Choose a primary goal: inference or prediction.
    Your current method section says logistic regression will be used for both “prediction” and “cause-and-effect.” Those are related but not identical tasks.

    • If your primary goal is inference, the paper should ask: which tract characteristics are associated with a higher probability of being low access, holding other tract characteristics constant? Then your emphasis should be on coefficient estimates, marginal effects, confidence intervals, and interpretation.
    • If your primary goal is prediction, the paper should ask: how accurately can tract characteristics classify whether a tract is low access? Then your emphasis should be on out-of-sample performance such as AUC, precision, recall, and the confusion matrix.

    Given your research questions, I think inference is the stronger primary direction, with prediction as an optional secondary section. That framing matches your question about which socioeconomic factors are associated with low food access. If you do include a prediction section, keep the claims clearly predictive and avoid treating predictive accuracy as evidence of causality.

  • Be more precise about the outcome variable.
    The proposal currently defines low access as one-third of the population living beyond the distance threshold. The USDA definition used for LA1and10 is slightly more specific: a tract is flagged if at least 500 people or at least 33 percent of the population live more than 1 mile from the nearest supermarket in urban areas or more than 10 miles in rural areas. That detail matters, especially for smaller tracts.

    For the capstone paper:

    • State the exact outcome variable name and definition.
    • Make clear that your unit of analysis is the census tract.
    • Distinguish carefully between a low-access tract and a low-income, low-access tract. Those are not the same USDA concepts, and the variable choice changes the interpretation of your results.

  • Match the model to the structure of your data.
    This point is very important. If you use only one USDA release, then the data are essentially cross-sectional, and a standard cross-sectional logistic regression is appropriate. But if you combine multiple USDA releases for the same census tracts, then you are working with a short tract-level panel and should use a model that reflects that structure.

    Because your dependent variable is binary, I recommend the following:

    • If you have one cross section, use cross-sectional logistic regression.
    • If you have a tract-level panel across multiple years, use a correlated random effects logistic model rather than a simple pooled logit.

    A correlated random effects approach is attractive here because it allows you to model a binary outcome while also accounting for tract-specific unobserved heterogeneity that may be correlated with your regressors. It is also more practical than a fixed-effects logit in a short panel, since fixed-effects logit drops tracts with no within-tract change in the binary outcome.

  • Write out the model specification explicitly.
    If you build a tract-level panel, your capstone paper should write the model clearly. A useful specification is:

    \[ \Pr(\text{LA1and10}_{it} = 1 \mid \mathbf{X}_{it}, \bar{\mathbf{X}}_i, c_i) = \text{logit}^{-1} \left( \beta_0 + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\beta} + \bar{\mathbf{X}}_i'\boldsymbol{\delta} + c_i \right), \]

    where \(i\) indexes census tracts, \(t\) indexes years, \(\mathbf{X}_{it}\) contains time-varying tract characteristics, and \(\bar{\mathbf{X}}_i\) contains tract-level means of those time-varying regressors. Including these tract means is the key correlated-random-effects adjustment because it allows the tract-specific effect to be correlated with the observed covariates.

    In words, this lets you separate:

    • within-tract changes over time, and
    • between-tract differences in average characteristics.

    That distinction will make your interpretation much stronger.

    If your data remain cross-sectional, then remove the time index and estimate an ordinary logistic regression instead.

  • Use appropriate interpretation and evaluation tools for a binary model.
    Because your outcome is binary:

    • RMSE should not be the main performance metric for the model.
    • For inference, focus on marginal effects and their confidence intervals.
    • For prediction, use AUC, precision, recall, and the confusion matrix.

    Odds ratios are acceptable, but the capstone paper will be easier to read if you also translate the main results into predicted probabilities or average marginal effects. Also remember that this is tract-level analysis, so you should interpret results as differences across neighborhoods, not as claims about individual households.

  • Handle the urban and rural issue more carefully.
    This is one of the most important conceptual issues in your design. The LA1and10 outcome already uses different thresholds for urban and rural tracts. That means the Urban variable is partly built into the dependent variable itself. If you simply include Urban as an ordinary control in one pooled model, interpretation becomes awkward.

    Better options:

    • Run separate models for urban and rural tracts.
    • Or estimate a pooled model with interaction terms and interpret cautiously.

    Separate models may be the clearest choice for an undergraduate capstone paper because the mechanisms are likely different. In New York City, public transit and neighborhood density may matter most. In rural upstate counties, vehicle access and long travel distances may matter more.

  • Be careful about which independent variables are mechanically related to the outcome.
    This is especially important for vehicle variables. The Atlas includes some measures that combine vehicle access and store distance. Those should not be used as predictors if LA1and10 is the outcome, because they overlap mechanically with the definition of low access.

    For the capstone paper:

    • Use a pure vehicle availability measure, not a “low access and no vehicle” measure.
    • Use race and ethnicity as shares of tract population rather than raw counts, since tract population sizes differ.
    • If you include multiple race-share variables, leave one group out as the reference category to avoid perfect collinearity.
    • Poverty rate and median family income may be highly correlated, so check that early and decide whether both belong in the same model.

  • Correct and document the data-vintage issue more carefully.
    Your report says the dataset is limited to 2010 census data and 2019 food sources. That is partly right, but the current USDA Atlas is actually a mixed-vintage dataset:

    • the tract geography and some population allocation pieces come from the 2010 Census,
    • income and vehicle access measures come from the 2014-18 American Community Survey,
    • rural and urban status uses the 2019 urban area definition,
    • and store locations come from 2019 store directories.

    Some demographic counts in the Atlas still trace back to 2010 census tract counts. So the capstone paper should explicitly document the source year for each major variable rather than treating the whole dataset as one year. This also means your conclusions describe late-2010s food access patterns, not current conditions.

  • Add a spatial and descriptive section before the regression.
    Your paper will be much stronger if readers first see where low-access tracts are and how they differ descriptively from other tracts. Before the model, I recommend:

    • a New York map of census tracts colored by LA1and10,
    • a table comparing low-access and non-low-access tracts on poverty, income, no-vehicle share, and urban or rural status,
    • and one simple figure showing low-access rates by county group or by urban versus rural tracts.

    These descriptive pieces will make the later regression results much easier to interpret.

  • Think about spatial dependence and clustered structure.
    Nearby tracts are not truly independent. Tracts within the same county or metro area share infrastructure, transit systems, land use patterns, and retailer networks. At minimum, consider standard errors clustered by county. If you estimate a tract-level panel, you may also want county or regional controls to absorb broader area characteristics.

    If prediction becomes a major part of the project, a purely random train/test split may overstate model performance because nearby tracts are similar. A county-based or metro-based validation split would be more convincing.

Questions to Think About as You Refine Your capstone paper

You do not need to answer all of these, but they may help you sharpen your analysis and policy narrative.

1. Cross Section or Panel

  • Are you using only one Atlas release, or will you combine multiple releases for the same census tracts?
  • If you combine releases, which variables truly vary over time and which are mostly fixed by construction?

2. Urban and Rural Mechanisms

  • Would it be cleaner to run separate urban and rural models, given that the USDA outcome itself uses different distance rules?
  • Are the strongest predictors of low access the same in New York City as they are in rural western or northern New York?

3. Variable Construction

  • Is your vehicle variable a pure no-car measure, or is it already combined with distance to stores? If it is the latter, it overlaps too much with the outcome.
  • Will you use race and ethnicity as shares, and how will you choose the omitted reference group?

4. Interpretation

  • In a correlated random effects model, are your main results coming from changes within tracts over time, or from persistent differences across tracts?
  • If poverty rate is strongly associated with low food access, what mechanism do you have in mind: lower retailer incentives, transportation barriers, residential segregation, zoning, or something else?

5. Policy Implications

  • If low vehicle access turns out to be the strongest predictor, does that point more toward transportation solutions than store-siting solutions?
  • Are there high-income but low-access rural tracts in New York? Those would be interesting cases because they may require a very different policy response than low-income urban tracts.
